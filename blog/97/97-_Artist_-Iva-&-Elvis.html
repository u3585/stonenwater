Project: Art & Market

 

Project: Art & Market The project we are working on as a part of SAP2009 is currently under a working title "Art & Market". It consists of several stages, layers if you like, most important being two public forums and the publication. Trough the course of the time spent at the residency we are conducting a research on two related topics: "Artist Contracts & Artists Rights" and "Gentrification & Public Art". As the outcome of the research we write a seminar which is used as basis for public forums. We use both the texts and forum documentation, as well as materials relevant to the topics for the publication we are working on. We are interested in the social and institutional networks and relations that determine and contain art. In this given work we are specifically interested in the relationships between art and the capital; which role does art occupy in the "market" and how or rather whether "artistic values" can be transferable into monetary values. We want to question art's ability to act as a political agent rather then engage in the processes of generating capital. Focusing on public art we want to discuss its' ways of conveying meaning. It is our belief that the change of physical space/location; moving from a gallery (art space) to the outdoor location (public space) doesn't make art more understandable to the general public, nor it affects its' political potential necessarily. By organizing public forums and creating a publication, as well as conducting research, we are using the institutional frame of artist residency i.e. gallery space, as a tool for generating and acquiring knowledge about the relations of capital and arts, processes of gentrification as well as histories of Seoksu market and public arts of Anyang/Korea. By doing this we hope we are playing a role (a small one but nevertheless) in these processes by disseminating ideas we value within the scope of these issues, and also learning more about the place that is our temporary location. The project we are realizing here is a part of a larger work in progress we plan to further after the residency, each time infused with the specifics of the location/context it is to be produced within.

 

프로젝트: 아트 & 마켓

SAP2009 석수아트프로젝트의 일환으로 현재 우리는 “아트 & 마켓 Art & Market”이라는 제목의 프로젝트를 진행하고 있다. 이 프로젝트는 몇 단계로 구성되어 있는데, 가장 중요한 부분은 두 번의 공개 포럼의 개최와 출판물 제작이다. 레지던스 기간 동안 우리는 두 개의 연관된 주제, 즉 “예술 계약 & 예술가의 권리 Art Contracts & Artists Rights”와 “젠트리피케이션과 공공미술 Gentrification & Public Art”에 관한 리서치를 진행했다. 이 리서치의 결과를 우리는 텍스트로 정리하여 공개 포럼의 기초자료로 사용하고, 이 텍스트와 공개 포럼의 기록 그리고 연구 주제와 관련된 자료들을 모아 출판물로 제작할 것이다.

우리는 예술을 정의 내리고 둘러싸고 있는 사회적, 제도적 네트워크와 관계들에 관심을 가지고 있다. 이번 프로젝트에서 우리는 특히 예술과 자본 사이의 관계에 중점을 두었다. 즉, 예술이 ‘시장 market’에서 차지하는 역할이나 ‘예술적 가치’가 금전적 가치로 전환되는 것이 어떻게 가능한 지에 대해 관심을 갖는다. 우리는 자본 생성의 과정에 관여하는 것 보다 정치적인 에이전트로서 역할 하는 예술의 가능성에 대해 질문하고자 한다. 또한 공공미술에 포커스를 두면서 이런 미술이 의미를 전달하는 방법에 대해 논의하고자 한다. 갤러리(예술 공간 art space)에서 야외 공간(공공 장소 public space)으로 이동하는 물리적인 공간과 장소의 변화 자체가 일반 대중에게 예술을 이해하기 더 쉽게 하거나 예술의 정치적인 가능성에 필연적으로 영향을 미치는 것은 아니라고 생각한다.

공개 포럼을 개최하고 출판물을 제작하거나 리서치를 수행하면서 우리는 아티스트 레시던시 즉, 갤러리 스페이스라는 제도적 프레임을 사용하는데, 이것은 예술과 자본의 관계, 젠트리피케이션 Gentrification의 과정 그리고 석수시장의 역사와 안양/한국의 공공미술에 대한 지식을 생성하고 습득하기 위한 도구로써 사용된다. 이를 통해 이런 주제의 범위 내에서 가치 있는 아이디어를 전파하고 우리가 임시로 거주하고 있는 이 공간에 대해 배우는 과정 속에서 (미약함에도 불구하고) 작은 역할을 하고자 희망한다.

우리가 여기서 실천한 프로젝트는 레지던시 이후에도 계속해서 진행될 큰 규모의 과정형 작업의 일부분이고, 작업이 진행될 때마다 매 장소/컨텍스트의 특수한 상황들이 작업에 주입된다.

[elvis - Previous work] Installation "Scars"

 

(translation of one of the texts used in the scars)

 

One of the ways to get out of class was to unscrew the screw on the sharpener, take the blade and cut yourself. After that it was enough to show the blood to the teacher and she would send us to put the bandage on. Later as I was watching the TV at home, dad’s friend made a comment in joke, something like “Did you fall on a barbeque or something?”. So my mum found out and concluded that if I’m capable of something like that that I might also start taking drugs as well.

 

​the concept

 

The scars are fragments of a body one can identify with because they bring corporeality into concrete context of time and space that also has its place in one’s memory. A wound is the temporality of the body and in the same time also a subjects projection. Trough “Scars” my engagement with the corporeality and memory is embodied in cuts; here I come, here I come!

 

Scars are as well embodiments of absence, absence of occurrences, they say; here the body – “was”, it is corporeality in past simple. I want to portray trough this work a certain absent referent of the scar, topography of the scar – the anecdote.

 

I have cut trough the mass of the gallery wall, short anecdotes (texts) that tell the events which led to emergence of the scars. In this way the scars into the “body” of the gallery were made that are in the same time also the scars on my body. And trough absence of the visual image of the actual scars the viewer is the one that bridges the body of the gallery and the body of the artist. The change of context of the emergence of scars into a new art context destabilizes the relationship of the scars and my personal experience – life. In the work “Scars” I am engaged with a certain pathetic relationship of the artistic subject and the gallery space – the institution. After textual drill flutes in the wall were made I filled them with light gray plaster so the texts functions as inlay in the matter of the gallery wall and in this way literally inscribe (the ephemeral) presence of the subject artist – permanently into the body of the gallery – institution/tradition. Individual texts, “Scars” are somewhat dislocated around all four wall surfaces circling the space of the gallery. The work is also playing with the idea of searching for ones own identity within the institutions/systems of culture, specifically the art system, relations of power etc.

 

Installation “Scars” functions as long term intervention in the body of the gallery literally “leaving trace” in the institution of art (even though the surface of the wall will be covered with paint, the scars will stay present within the wall mass and under the paint as material (plaster) and identity).

[elvis - previous work] Installation "Sutra"

 

The gallery installation consisted of photo - documentation of the intervention into advertising space (public) and the light display with the sintagm that is adopted from the title of the essay by Boris Groys "Back from the future".

 

​Concept

 

...Boris Groys interpretes a process of western modernization of post-communist countries as that of moving against time, as if coming “Back from the future**". In former Yugoslavia during the 50’s the photographic documentation of restoration ( in publications “Zeljezara Zenica”...) was used to promote the ideas of socialism. Today these photographs seem analogous to the sintagm “Back from the future” . In an encounter with this kind of photography today, we realize the lack of vision of the future or ideology on which current vision of the future could rely on. Even though the scene from the photograph I’m using, made by Toso Dabac , could remind us of dark movie visions of the future (Metropolis...) in the given context it would undoubtedly be seen as the promotion of socialism. I purposely used a photograph of ambivalent nature so to create a displaced socio-political identity specific to post-communist countries in current transitional state. To underline this I posted the photograph on a billboard - a promotional space of consumerist society, while the photograph is a promotion of socialism, hence a billboard surface becomes a place where two different ideologies encounter...

 

​

*The title of the work is adopted title of the work by Mladen Stilinovic "Sutra (tomorrow)"

 

** "Back from the future" Boris Groys (from the catalogue of the exhibition "The art of eastern Europe" Arteast collection 2000+)

 

Photograph of Toso Dabac was handed over with curtsey of the archive Toso Dabac, under Museum of contemporary art, Zagreb for the realization of the work "Sutra (tomorow)".

 

Posting of the billboard was photographed by Petar Bunić

Out of several texts I have extracted explicit misogynist statements from some of the most influential male authors and used them as public posters. The quote extracted from a book and the intellectual sphere, written with thick pink letters on a public poster emphasizes the conflict between the statement and the way it is presented. I am focusing on the subconscious implantation of stereotypes through education, specific for the local situation where the never literature that throws a different light at the problem is barely infiltrating in the educational system.

 

The project started off as an anonymous intervention by putting up posters in the public spaces throughout the city of Zagreb and creating a web page it was named after which was created to contextualize the work and communicate with the public. It provoked a lot of interest from the internet forum communities and as it was exhibited under my name the media became very interested. It was peculiar how the attitude towards the project rapidly changed when it got attributed to a particular person.

[iva - previous work] The Kissing Piece

 

This work was conceived in order to question a specific performance. Realized as an intervention made directly ‘on the work’ – in this case the performer’s body as she was performing - not in retrospect. Therefore it was a critical statement realized through artistic means which enabled me with an immediate response but at the same time I risked an unpredictable reaction.

 

To describe what the piece is about I have to focus on somebody else’s work first - the performance I intervened in: The performer stands still and quiet for half an hour, five days in a row, in a gallery space open to visitors, and the visitors are welcomed to interact with her as encouraged in the invitation. Thereby it is suggested that she is revealing herself to the public. Since the performance was done in a familiar environment (the gallery space inside the art academy) with only a few visitors, most of them being artist’s friends, I tried to provoke a shock and see how the performance would develop. I have asked the performer to allow me to create a work of mine by participating (as well as contributing to her participatory performance) to what she had agreed. I have kissed her as I was encouraged by the invitation, among other suggestions. My action was received as an assault and I was first denied right to publicly exhibit the piece (by the performer), and later we reached a compromise, by which I was allowed to exhibit but only under the terms of concealing her identity. In my opinion, the circumstances in which the performance was staged were at odds with a possibility of a significant interaction and this is what it was declaratively aiming at - opening to the public. The idea itself seemed intensely reminiscent of Marina Abramović’s Rthym 0 where Abramović exposed herself to the public, an action that has almost finished with severe consequences.

 

In body art and performance practices the work is not in what you see (one person standing) but in the whole series of events and opinions it is able to produce. I believe that just as my piece depended on hers so did her piece benefited from my intervention.

[Art&Market] Art Contracts & Artists' Rights - 1. Forum

 

photographs provided with courtesy of Kim Sungsoo

 

​Questions and answers transcription from the forum:

 

​Mr. Park: You mentioned different kinds of 'censorship' within two contexts; within communism and the free-market? Can you elaborate more on this difference and why do you think the 'censorship' that is present now is more profound than the one of communist time?

 

​Iva: First I must say that we were talking about the communism of Yugoslavia which was a moderate form of that regime model. Yugoslavia was semi-open towards the West and was not part of the East block but of the Unaligned and it didn’t practice this regime in the same manner as countries like for instance USSR or North Korea. It is not true that it was like that for each and every artist. The point is however that while you are not included in the market you are not censored, same as you were not censored in communism as long you were not within the scope of the Party's attention. So when you reach the position that somebody is actually paying attention at what you are doing, when I say somebody I mean somebody in the position of power, be that the government or capital, then there is a danger of being censored.

 

​Elvis: I would like to add to the answer; in the statement from artist Mladen Stilinović* I mentioned, I believe he made a comparison of Yugoslavian communist regime with free - market in general and not with the transitory local version currently present in Croatia. His experience is that of an internationally active artist so he is talking from that perspective I believe.

 

There is one important thing to add in regard to Yugoslavian regime and that is the state of art. The art in Yugoslavian communist era is not the kind of art you might imagine in a communist state - social realism and such. I mean there were public sculptures done in this way, especially until 1948 but later, even the sculptures that were commisioned by the state, were done in a modernist, abstract or semi-abstract manner.

 

In any case, the discussion is essentially about the difference of private and public funding. While private funding is in most cases openly working in the interest of the subject that provides the money, the public funds, like public spaces are at least declaratively, still in the interest of the wider public. These spaces are still open for discussion ... I mean It depends but that was maybe what the artist had in mind when he talked about economic censorship.

 

​Choy Ko Eun: As a scenario writer I feel, after looking trough the contents of the "Art Contracts & Artists Rights", that clauses included in the contracts do not actually protect the artists but define the rules, construct the framework within which artists must create their work. In a way that limits their creativity. Many times, as a scenario writer I apply to companies, but they deny the application saying it doesn't follow the specific guidelines of the contract. So when I ask them how that could be fixed they say that no matter how many times I hand them my work, as long as it doesn't follow the guidelines, they will not look at it.

 

So the question is weather these contracts actually protect the artist's rights or hinder them from their personal creative process? And another question is; weather it is possible to see the progress in which these contracts actually protect the artist instead of just defining or making guidelines for artists to follow?

 

​Iva: I am not sure I understood the question. When you had to fill in the form, when applying for the job, you did not fill it in correctly so you were not accepted?

 

​Choy Ko Eun: The contents of the work did not follow the guidelines of the contracts. So the question is weather these contracts protect the artist right's or weather artists actually often don't produce as much because of so many constraints and limitations present in the contracts.

 

​Iva: The contracts we were talking about were drafted by artists or by an art dealer that was very interested in the artists’ benefit, financial in this case, but they were really drafted for the sake of artists. So I don't think that it is the same issue at stake. They were drafted because the system is not regulated, or if it is regulated than it is regulated by the other party, like the companies you mentioned, which tried to regulate it in their own interest.

 

​Elvis: Not to idealize these contracts; they mostly work for those artists who are already successful when they want to protect their rights. Not to suggest you are not successful or anything [smiles], but when Daniel Buren drafted this it was at the beginning of his career and he did not menage to use it for a number of years until he started to sell his work. It addresses problems of artists that are already functioning decently within the market, whose work is already circulating. If you are not in the position to say no, then this contract obviously cannot help you.

 

​Iva: Ussualy the galleries already have their contracts drafted and in many cases these are not beneficial for the artists. Not to name anyone but number of galleries in Croatia work with contracts under which you have to give at least 50% of the money you get for the artwork. Even if you sell your work on your own, trough acquaintances or whatever, with no help of the gallery whatsoever you have to give the percentage to them, but according to the contract they don't actually have the obligation to sell any amount of your works. So basically you have obliged yourself to them but they did not do the same to you. And because these galleries are not always successful it often happens they don't sell much, or anything. So there is no risk for them thanks to their contracts. But if you have your own contract it might be a good thing - of course if you are in a position in which they might actually sign it. And talking about the movie industry, it is a different field, so I can not really speak about that.

 

​Michelle's boyfriend: As a photographer speaking in terms of photography - these days, it is kind of funny having this conversation because there has been so much of "letting go", so much you have to accept having to put your art online. It is going to go out there and you have to sort of take your hands of, not so much for the commodity of money, so much as for exposure. There is some value in that and I was just wondering what you think of that?

 

​Elvis: There are more then one different strategies/approaches to the matters regarding digital content in general. Like free software initiatives, GNU licenses, file sharing communities etc, and what these people are saying, not to confuse file sharing with GNU licenses, could be understood as "Death of author" notion. They are not regarding the author as "self sufficient" subject, but culture as the author. What they are saying is that if we, in fact, inherit so much from the culture, why then not give it back to the culture by not closing or concealing your work - be it software source code or photography and video. But nevertheless the question remains how to maintain your material needs if you follow this logic. There is undoubtedly a lot of romanticism present in the definition of the culture as a practice of sharing but this doesn't solve some basic needs of the profesional content creators.

 

Many authors embrace the practice of protecting their work by technically limiting further use; making available online only the low resolution/low quality of images/videos so if someone wants full quality of a certain image/video they have to buy it. But you may also say in response that you would easily give high resolution in exchange for free access to information.

 

But for me it is also interesting that the generation of artists that was ready to discard the notion of artistic genius and authorship on theoretical level did not engage in establishing different models of making income as artists and were so proprietary in regard to their authorship.

 

​*During the reading of the text Elvis Krstulovic praphrased the folowing line from the conversation with Mladen Stilinovic as a digression:

 

“There are different grounds on which you are censored in a communist regime and that of free - market. While in communism you are censored on ideological grounds, if your statement opposes the official ideology, the censorship present in capitalist regime is the economical one. If it doesn't sell it is just disregarded. And it is my belief that the later is more profound because in the end it works as a self censorship as well.”

Interview with Chaneung Park and Woon-Gi Min

 

​Hi everyone here we are again. We wanted to show you some material from our process. In last two weeks we have conducted two interviews with directors of Alternative spaces in Anyang and Incheon - Stone&Water and Space Beam. We had a wonderful learning experience in this two interviews and we plan to use them as some of the sources for our newspapers/publication we are currently working on.
